\begin{abstract}
We present a theory-oriented study of residual reinforcement learning for robotic ball bouncing under uncertain simulation physics. The controller combines a finite-state-machine and inverse-kinematics baseline (FSM+IK) with a learned Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) residual that provides bounded joint-space corrections. We compare nominal SAC training on fixed physics against robust SAC training with per-episode domain randomization over mass, friction, restitution, and dissipation parameters. Experiments use 20-episode deterministic evaluations at checkpoints (500k, 750k, 1M training steps) under nominal and randomized test physics in the Drake simulator, plus 500-episode cross-simulator evaluations in MuJoCo. Nominal SAC achieves the strongest absolute performance in Drake (482 mean hits under nominal physics; 328 under randomized), while robust SAC exhibits better relative robustness trends at later checkpoints (+25.9\% at 750k and +48.5\% at 1M from nominal to randomized evaluation). In MuJoCo sim-to-sim transfer, all policies experience substantial degradation, but the robust SAC policy retains the best relative stability. We further analyze how the residual scale parameter governs the degree of RL correction injected on top of the baseline, finding that a narrow optimal range ($\alpha \approx 0.01$) yields peak hit counts while larger or smaller values degrade performance. Code and artifacts are public at \url{https://github.com/MG-05/ping-pong-domain-randomization}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Dynamic ball-bouncing control on a robotic arm is a challenging benchmark because high-frequency contact events amplify small model errors. In this project, policy quality is not determined solely by nominal return; it is also determined by resilience under physically plausible perturbations. This motivates a robustness-first framing.

Our system uses a hybrid architecture:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
    \item an analytic inner loop (FSM+IK) for structured intercept and strike behavior,
    \item a learned residual outer loop (SAC) for corrective adaptation.
\end{itemize}

This decomposition supports a rigorous question: when does residual learning improve performance, and how does domain randomization change generalization behavior?

We evaluate three research questions:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{RQ1 (Absolute Performance):} Can residual SAC exceed the hand-engineered baseline?
    \item \textbf{RQ2 (Robustness):} Does domain-randomized training improve behavior under perturbed physics?
    \item \textbf{RQ3 (Stability):} What do variance and survival metrics reveal about policy reliability?
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\textbf{Entropy-regularized RL.}
SAC optimizes return plus policy entropy, giving strong continuous-control performance and improved training stability~\cite{haarnoja2018sac}.

\textbf{Continuous-control actor-critic lineage.}
Deterministic actor-critic methods such as DDPG~\cite{lillicrap2015ddpg} show the utility of off-policy replay-based learning in robotics, but can be brittle under noise and hyperparameter shifts.

\textbf{Domain randomization.}
Randomizing simulator dynamics can reduce overfitting to a single simulator instance and improve out-of-distribution robustness~\cite{tobin2017domainrand,peng2018sim2real}.

\textbf{Robust and residual RL.}
Robust RL emphasizes performance under perturbations~\cite{pinto2017robust}; residual RL combines structured control priors with learned corrections for safety and sample efficiency~\cite{johannink2019residualrl}.

\textbf{Practical implementation framework.}
Stable-Baselines3 provides reproducible, standard RL training infrastructure that we leverage for SAC training and evaluation orchestration~\cite{raffin2021sb3}.

\section{Theoretical Background}
\subsection{MDP and Residual Action Parameterization}
Let $\mathcal{M}=(\mathcal{S},\mathcal{A},P,r,\gamma)$ denote the Markov decision process~\cite{sutton2018rl}, where $\mathcal{S}$ is the state space, $\mathcal{A}$ is the action space, $P(s'|s,a)$ is the transition kernel, $r(s,a)$ is the reward function, and $\gamma \in [0,1)$ is the discount factor.
Here, $s_t\in\mathbb{R}^{20}$ contains the robot joint positions and velocities (14 dimensions) plus the ball Cartesian position and velocity (6 dimensions), and $a_t\in[-1,1]^7$ is a normalized residual action where each component corresponds to one of the seven KUKA iiwa joint axes.

Baseline and residual are combined to produce the commanded joint position vector as follows:
\[
q_t^{\mathrm{cmd}}
=\Pi_{\mathcal{Q}}\!\left(q_t^{\mathrm{base}}+\alpha\,\delta\,\mathrm{clip}(a_t,-1,1)\right),
\]
where:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
    \item $q_t^{\mathrm{cmd}} \in \mathbb{R}^7$ is the final joint-position command sent to the robot at time step $t$,
    \item $q_t^{\mathrm{base}} \in \mathbb{R}^7$ is the baseline command produced by the FSM+IK controller (Section~\ref{sec:fsm_architecture}),
    \item $\alpha \in [0,1]$ is the \emph{residual scale}, controlling how much of the learned correction is injected (Section~\ref{sec:residual_scale}),
    \item $\delta = 0.15$ rad is the maximum residual magnitude per joint,
    \item $\mathrm{clip}(a_t,-1,1)$ clamps each component of the policy output to $[-1,1]$,
    \item $\Pi_{\mathcal{Q}}$ projects the result onto the feasible joint-limit set $\mathcal{Q} = \{q : q^{\min} \le q \le q^{\max}\}$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{SAC Objective}
SAC solves an entropy-regularized objective
\[
J(\pi)=\mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim\pi}\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty}\gamma^t\left(r_t+\beta\mathcal{H}(\pi(\cdot|s_t))\right)\right],
\]
where $\tau = (s_0,a_0,r_0,s_1,\ldots)$ is a trajectory sampled under policy $\pi$, $r_t = r(s_t,a_t)$ is the per-step reward, $\beta > 0$ is the entropy temperature (auto-tuned in our implementation), and $\mathcal{H}(\pi(\cdot|s_t)) = -\mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi}[\log \pi(a|s_t)]$ is the policy entropy at state $s_t$.

The soft Bellman operator defines the soft Q-function $Q^\pi$ and soft value function $V^\pi$:
\begin{align*}
Q^\pi(s_t,a_t) &= r_t+\gamma\,\mathbb{E}_{s_{t+1} \sim P}\!\left[V^\pi(s_{t+1})\right], \\
V^\pi(s) &= \mathbb{E}_{a\sim\pi}\!\left[Q^\pi(s,a)-\beta\log\pi(a|s)\right].
\end{align*}

\subsection{Why Residual SAC Is Appropriate for This Task}
Robotic ball bouncing involves known geometric structure (intercept timing and IK). Learning full control from scratch is unnecessary and increases exploration burden. Residual SAC addresses a narrower problem: improving a feasible baseline policy via bounded corrections.

The feasible joint-limit set $\mathcal{Q} = \{q \in \mathbb{R}^7 : q^{\min}_i \le q_i \le q^{\max}_i, \; i=1,\ldots,7\}$ is the Cartesian product of per-joint intervals defined by the KUKA iiwa hardware limits, further tightened by a configurable deviation bound around the home configuration.

\begin{remark}
Because residual exploration is bounded by $\alpha\delta$ per joint and then projected onto $\mathcal{Q}$, the residual policy cannot directly violate joint-position constraints. A full proof is given in Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Robustness Under Transition Shift}
Let $P_{\mathrm{nom}}$ and $P_{\mathrm{rand}}$ denote the nominal and randomized transition kernels respectively, where $P_{\mathrm{nom}}(s'|s,a)$ governs state transitions under fixed (default) physics parameters, and $P_{\mathrm{rand}}(s'|s,a)$ governs transitions under per-episode randomized physics. Training only on $P_{\mathrm{nom}}$ may maximize nominal return while suffering \emph{performance degradation}---a drop in mean hit count and survival rate---under $P_{\mathrm{rand}}$. Domain-randomized training approximates risk minimization over the distribution of physics perturbations, specifically minimizing the expected drop in cumulative reward across the randomization envelope.

\subsection{SAC Training Dynamics in Residual Control}
For this project, SAC optimization is best interpreted as learning a correction distribution over a moving baseline controller output. Unlike standard end-to-end policy learning, the action semantics are locally corrective: each action dimension perturbs one joint around a feasible IK-informed command. This affects critic learning in two ways.

First, replay buffer support is naturally concentrated around physically plausible commands, because the baseline controller prevents the robot from reaching states far from the kinematic manifold of valid arm configurations (i.e., joint configurations reachable through the FSM's IK-planned trajectories). Second, value approximation error can still accumulate due to contact discontinuities (ball-paddle impacts), leading to the non-monotonic checkpoint behavior observed in our results.

In actor-critic form, SAC alternates:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{critic updates}: regress soft Bellman targets under replayed transitions;
    \item \textbf{actor updates}: maximize soft value while preserving entropy;
    \item \textbf{target updates}: Polyak averaging controlled by $\tau = 0.005$.
\end{itemize}
Because the reward combines sparse event terms (new hit bonus of $+5.0$) and dense shaping terms (alive bonus of $+0.01$ per step, apex-tracking bonus), critic targets mix abrupt and smooth components. This mixed structure plausibly contributes to temporary mismatch between the critic's value estimates and the true returns at intermediate checkpoints (e.g., the 750k dip in Figure~\ref{fig:learning}).

\subsection{Entropy Temperature and Exploration Interpretation}
In SAC, entropy regularization controls exploration intensity. In a residual-control setting, higher entropy does not mean arbitrary behavior in task space; it means diverse local perturbations around structured baseline commands. This can be advantageous when baseline timing is approximately correct but contact quality requires adaptation.

However, entropy-driven exploration can still produce transiently suboptimal residual patterns before converging to better correction strategies. The 500k $\rightarrow$ 750k dip followed by 1M rebound in both nominal and robust tracks (Figure~\ref{fig:learning}) is consistent with such reorganization.

\subsection{Critic and Actor Losses}
SAC alternates stochastic gradient steps on the twin critic loss and the actor loss. The critic loss for parameters $\phi$ is:
\begin{align*}
\mathcal{L}_Q(\phi) &=
\mathbb{E}_{(s,a,r,s')\sim\mathcal{D}}
\left[\left(Q_\phi(s,a)-y(r,s')\right)^2\right],\\
y(r,s') &= r + \gamma\left(
\min_{i\in\{1,2\}}Q_{\bar\phi_i}(s',a')
 - \beta\log\pi_\theta(a'|s')
\right),
\end{align*}
where $\mathcal{D}$ is the replay buffer of past transitions $(s,a,r,s')$, $Q_{\bar\phi_i}$ are the two target critic networks with parameters $\bar\phi_i$ updated via Polyak averaging, $a'\sim\pi_\theta(\cdot|s')$ is an action sampled from the current policy at the next state, and $\beta$ is the entropy temperature. The actor objective for parameters $\theta$ is:
\[
\mathcal{L}_\pi(\theta)=
\mathbb{E}_{s\sim\mathcal{D},\,a\sim\pi_\theta}
\left[\beta\log\pi_\theta(a|s)-\min_i Q_{\phi_i}(s,a)\right].
\]

\subsection{Distributional Robustness Perspective}
Let $\xi = (\text{ball mass}, \text{friction}, \text{restitution}, \text{dissipation}, \ldots)$ denote the vector of randomized physics parameters sampled from a distribution $\mathcal{P}_\Xi$ defined by the ranges in Table~\ref{tab:dr_ranges}. Nominal training optimizes expected return under a single fixed parameter vector $\xi_0$:
\[
\max_\pi \; \mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim P_{\xi_0},\pi}\!\left[R(\tau)\right],
\]
where $R(\tau) = \sum_{t=0}^{T} \gamma^t r_t$ is the discounted cumulative reward along trajectory $\tau$, and $P_{\xi_0}$ is the transition kernel induced by the nominal physics $\xi_0$.

Robust (domain-randomized) training instead optimizes the expected return averaged over the physics distribution:
\[
\max_\pi \; \mathbb{E}_{\xi\sim\mathcal{P}_\Xi}\mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim P_{\xi},\pi}\!\left[R(\tau)\right].
\]
This does not guarantee worst-case optimality, but it better aligns optimization with deployment where physics are uncertain.

\begin{proposition}[Expected-risk interpretation]
\label{prop:expected_risk}
If test-time dynamics are sampled from $\mathcal{P}_\Xi$, then robust-domain-randomized training is an unbiased Monte Carlo estimator of expected return under that distribution (up to function-approximation error and finite sample effects).
\end{proposition}

\textit{Practical implication:}
robust SAC can improve average behavior across perturbations even when it does not maximize peak nominal performance. A full proof is provided in Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}.

\section{Methods and Implementation}
\subsection{Environment and Timing}
The environment (\texttt{src/envs/residual\_env.py}) runs physics at $1$\,ms time steps (the Drake simulator's internal integration step) and applies RL control actions at $10$\,ms intervals. This 10:1 time-scale separation preserves contact fidelity in the physics engine while keeping policy inference tractable---the RL agent only needs to produce an action every $10$\,ms rather than every physics step.

\subsection{Observation, Action, and Interfaces}
\textbf{Observation (20-D):}
The observation vector at each control step is
\[
o_t=\bigl[\underbrace{q_{1:7}}_{\text{joint pos.}},\;\underbrace{\dot{q}_{1:7}}_{\text{joint vel.}},\;\underbrace{p_b^x,p_b^y,p_b^z}_{\text{ball pos.}},\;\underbrace{v_b^x,v_b^y,v_b^z}_{\text{ball vel.}}\bigr],
\]
where $q_{1:7} \in \mathbb{R}^7$ are the KUKA iiwa joint angles (rad), $\dot{q}_{1:7} \in \mathbb{R}^7$ are the corresponding joint velocities (rad/s), $p_b \in \mathbb{R}^3$ is the ball center-of-mass position in the world frame (m), and $v_b \in \mathbb{R}^3$ is the ball linear velocity (m/s).

\textbf{Action (7-D):}
The policy outputs a normalized residual $a_t \in [-1,1]^7$, where each component $a_t^{(i)}$ maps to a bounded joint offset $\alpha \cdot \delta \cdot a_t^{(i)}$ on joint $i$ of the iiwa arm (with $\alpha = 0.5$ and $\delta = 0.15$\,rad by default).

At each control step (every $10$\,ms):
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
    \item Evaluate the FSM+IK controller (Section~\ref{sec:fsm_architecture}) to get the baseline joint command $q_t^{\mathrm{base}} \in \mathbb{R}^7$.
    \item Query the residual SAC policy for normalized action $a_t \in [-1,1]^7$ and compute the corrected command $q_t^{\mathrm{cmd}} = \Pi_{\mathcal{Q}}(q_t^{\mathrm{base}} + \alpha\delta\,\mathrm{clip}(a_t))$.
    \item Send $q_t^{\mathrm{cmd}}$ as a position command to the iiwa joint position controller (a Drake PD controller that tracks the desired joint positions by generating appropriate joint torques).
    \item Advance the Drake simulator by one control interval ($10$\,ms, i.e., 10 physics steps at $1$\,ms each) and compute the reward and termination signals.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Reward Construction}
\label{sec:reward}
The reward function at each control step combines four terms, designed to balance persistence, contact productivity, and bounce quality:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Alive bonus} ($r_{\text{alive}} = 0.01$): awarded at every step where the ball height $p_b^z$ remains above the minimum threshold $z_{\min} = 0.08$\,m.
    \item \textbf{Hit bonus} ($r_{\text{hit}} = 5.0$): awarded for each new debounced paddle-ball contact event, with a cooldown of 10 control steps ($100$\,ms) between consecutive hit detections to prevent double-counting a single contact.
    \item \textbf{Apex-tracking bonus}: $r_{\mathrm{apex}} \cdot \exp(-10 \cdot e_{\mathrm{apex}}^2)$, where $r_{\mathrm{apex}} = 2.0$ is the maximum apex reward and $e_{\mathrm{apex}} = |p_b^z - z_{\mathrm{target}}|$ is the absolute error between the ball's height at its trajectory apex and the target apex height $z_{\mathrm{target}} = 0.55$\,m. This bonus is triggered when the ball's vertical velocity crosses zero from positive to negative (i.e., the ball reaches its peak).
    \item \textbf{Drop penalty} ($r_{\text{drop}} = -5.0$): applied when the ball falls below $z_{\min}$, which also terminates the episode.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Training Configuration}
From \texttt{src/train\_rl.py}, SAC is configured with:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
    \item MLP policy with two hidden layers of 256 units each, $[256,256]$,
    \item learning rate $3\times10^{-4}$,
    \item batch size 256 transitions per gradient step,
    \item replay buffer capacity $10^5$ transitions,
    \item discount factor $\gamma=0.99$, target network Polyak rate $\tau=0.005$,
    \item residual scale $\alpha = 0.5$, maximum residual $\delta = 0.15$ rad.
\end{itemize}

Two training tracks are used:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Nominal SAC:} trained under fixed (default) physics parameters.
    \item \textbf{Robust SAC:} trained with per-episode domain randomization over the ranges in Table~\ref{tab:dr_ranges}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Domain Randomization Mechanism}
\texttt{src/utils/randomization.py} samples parameters uniformly from the ranges in Table~\ref{tab:dr_ranges} and writes randomized model/scenario files before each robust episode. This means perturbations alter actual simulation artifacts (not merely internal coefficients) and better reflect realistic structural variation in contact dynamics.

\section{Experimental Protocol}
\subsection{Hypotheses}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{H1:} Residual SAC can exceed FSM baseline hit counts.
    \item \textbf{H2:} Domain-randomized SAC improves relative perturbation robustness.
    \item \textbf{H3:} Learning dynamics are non-monotonic across checkpoints.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evaluation Procedure}
\texttt{src/evaluate.py} runs deterministic policy inference for 20 episodes per model-condition pair.
Evaluated checkpoints: 500k, 750k, 1M training steps.
Evaluated conditions: nominal and randomized physics.

\subsection{Metrics}
\begin{definition}[Mean hit performance]
For episode hits $h_e$ (the number of debounced paddle-ball contacts in episode $e$), $H=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{e=1}^{N}h_e$, where $N$ is the number of evaluation episodes.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Relative perturbation effect]
\[
\Delta(\%)=100\cdot\frac{H_{\mathrm{rand}}-H_{\mathrm{nom}}}{H_{\mathrm{nom}}},
\]
where $H_{\mathrm{rand}}$ and $H_{\mathrm{nom}}$ are the mean hits under randomized and nominal physics respectively. Positive $\Delta$ indicates the policy performs \emph{better} under randomized physics than under nominal.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Relative variability]
\[
\mathrm{CV}(\%)=100\cdot\frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{rand}}}{H_{\mathrm{rand}}},
\]
where $\sigma_{\mathrm{rand}}$ is the standard deviation of hit counts under randomized physics. Lower CV indicates more consistent performance across physics perturbations.
\end{definition}

\begin{assumption}[Controlled comparisons]
Within each evaluation block, horizon, deterministic inference mode, and episode count are fixed.
\end{assumption}

\section{Implementation Walkthrough}
\subsection{End-to-End Dataflow}
At runtime, one control iteration follows this sequence:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
    \item read simulator outputs: the iiwa state vector $[q_{1:7}, \dot{q}_{1:7}] \in \mathbb{R}^{14}$ (7 joint positions and 7 joint velocities of the KUKA iiwa arm), the ball state (position $p_b$ and velocity $v_b$), and the ball contact-force vector (6-D generalized forces used for hit detection);
    \item evaluate the FSM controller (Section~\ref{sec:fsm_architecture}) to generate $q_t^{\mathrm{base}}$;
    \item query residual SAC policy for normalized action $a_t \in [-1,1]^7$;
    \item map $a_t$ to bounded joint corrections: $\Delta q = \alpha \cdot \delta \cdot \mathrm{clip}(a_t)$ and add to $q_t^{\mathrm{base}}$;
    \item clamp to joint bounds via $\Pi_{\mathcal{Q}}$ and send the resulting position command $q_t^{\mathrm{cmd}}$ to the iiwa joint-position controller;
    \item advance simulation by one control interval (10\,ms) and compute reward/termination.
\end{enumerate}
This decomposition isolates high-level tactical behavior (intercept/strike logic) from low-level adaptation (residual correction), making failures easier to diagnose than in monolithic policies.

\subsection{Why the Observation Space Is Structured This Way}
The 20-D observation contains joint position/velocity ($q_{1:7}$, $\dot{q}_{1:7}$) and ball position/velocity ($p_b$, $v_b$). Conceptually, this is the minimum state needed to:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
    \item infer arm configuration and motion trend,
    \item infer ball flight state for near-term intercept correction,
    \item avoid overfitting to simulator-internal artifacts.
\end{itemize}
No image stream is used; this keeps the problem in a dynamics-control regime rather than perception-heavy policy learning.

\subsection{Reward Design Trade-offs}
The reward (Section~\ref{sec:reward}) blends sparse success events and dense shaping:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
    \item sparse hit bonuses ($r_{\text{hit}} = 5.0$) produce strong task signal but can destabilize critic learning when contacts are rare;
    \item alive ($r_{\text{alive}} = 0.01$) and apex shaping terms reduce reward sparsity but may create local optima (e.g., keeping short rallies alive rather than maximizing long-term hit count);
    \item drop penalties ($r_{\text{drop}} = -5.0$) discourage catastrophic failure but can overweight terminal events if improperly tuned.
\end{itemize}
In this project, the combined reward appears effective for discovering high-hit trajectories at 1M steps, but also exhibits checkpoint volatility, suggesting potential future reward-scaling work.

\subsection{Contact Event Debouncing and Measurement Semantics}
\label{sec:contact_debounce}
Hit counting uses force-thresholded contact detection: a hit is registered when the ball contact-force magnitude exceeds a threshold ($0.05$\,N) \emph{and} the ball is above a minimum height ($0.20$\,m), with a cooldown of 10 control steps ($100$\,ms) between consecutive detections. This matters for interpretation: ``hits'' are not simply every physics-step contact, but filtered events intended to approximate meaningful paddle-ball interactions, filtering out other ball contacts such as floor bounces or incidental collisions. Therefore, reported hit-rate differences can reflect both policy behavior and event-definition sensitivity, a point we include in validity threats.

\subsection{Training Infrastructure and Reproducibility}
The training loop uses a standard set of RL engineering controls:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
    \item checkpoint callback for temporal model snapshots,
    \item eval callback for periodic deterministic policy evaluation,
    \item monitor-wrapped environments for episodic metrics,
    \item fixed seeds to improve experiment repeatability.
\end{itemize}
These controls enable post-hoc analysis of learning dynamics such as the 750k performance dip.

\subsection{Domain Randomization as Scenario Generation}
Randomization is implemented by sampling parameters and writing randomized model/scenario files used at environment reset. This means perturbations alter actual simulation artifacts (not merely internal coefficients) and better reflect realistic structural variation in contact dynamics.

\subsection{FSM Controller Architecture}
\label{sec:fsm_architecture}
The baseline controller is a six-state finite state machine (Figure~\ref{fig:fsm_diagram}) implemented in \texttt{src/controllers/fsm\_controller.py}. Each state maps to a distinct phase of the ball-bouncing rally cycle:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{WAIT}: holds the home joint configuration $q_{\mathrm{home}} = [0, 0.6, 0, -1.75, 0, 1.0, 0]$ rad; monitors ball state. Transitions to PLAN when all of: (i) the ball is descending ($v_b^z < -0.12$ m/s), (ii) the ball is above minimum height ($p_b^z > 0.26$ m), (iii) the plan cooldown ($30$ ms since last plan) has elapsed, and (iv) a valid ballistic intercept exists at the target hit height.

    \item \textbf{PLAN}: predicts a ballistic intercept at the target hit height ($z_{\mathrm{hit}} = 0.38$ m) using projectile kinematics (see below). Computes the desired paddle pose and strike normal, then solves IK for three waypoints (prehit, hit, follow-through). Transitions to PREHIT on IK success, or to RECOVER if IK fails for any waypoint.

    \item \textbf{PREHIT}: interpolates the arm from its current configuration toward the prehit waypoint using linear joint-space interpolation. May replan at $40$ ms intervals if time permits (replan cutoff: $40$ ms before strike). Transitions to STRIKE when the current time reaches the scheduled strike start time $t_{\mathrm{strike}} = t_{\mathrm{hit}} - t_{\mathrm{lead}}$, where $t_{\mathrm{lead}} \ge 60$ ms is the strike lead time. Transitions to RECOVER if the ball passes the predicted hit time without striking.

    \item \textbf{STRIKE}: executes a timed linear interpolation through prehit$\to$hit$\to$follow waypoints. Detects contact via force threshold ($> 0.05$ N with ball above workspace floor) or ball velocity reversal (vertical velocity sign change from negative to positive, indicating an upward rebound). Transitions to FOLLOW\_THROUGH on hit detection or after the strike window expires ($t_{\mathrm{tail}} \ge 50$ ms after $t_{\mathrm{hit}}$).

    \item \textbf{FOLLOW\_THROUGH}: holds the follow-through pose for $\sim\!100$ ms, then transitions to WAIT.

    \item \textbf{RECOVER}: returns to home pose $q_{\mathrm{home}}$ when planning fails or a strike is missed; transitions to WAIT once joint position error is below $0.45$ rad and joint velocity magnitude is below $0.25$ rad/s relative to the home configuration.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Ballistic intercept prediction.}
The PLAN state uses classical projectile kinematics under gravity to predict where the ball will reach the target hit height. Given ball position $p_b$ and velocity $v_b$ at the current time, the vertical trajectory under gravity $g = 9.81$ m/s$^2$ is modeled as:
\[
z(t) = p_b^z + v_b^z \cdot t - \tfrac{1}{2} g \, t^2.
\]
The FSM solves $z(t) = z_{\mathrm{hit}}$ via the quadratic formula to find the time-to-intercept $\tau_{\mathrm{hit}}$. Among real positive roots in the valid horizon ($60$ ms $\le \tau_{\mathrm{hit}} \le 1.1$ s), it selects the earliest root where the ball is still descending. The predicted 3D intercept position is then:
\[
p_{\mathrm{hit}} = p_b + v_b \cdot \tau_{\mathrm{hit}} + \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ -\tfrac{1}{2}g\,\tau_{\mathrm{hit}}^2 \end{bmatrix},
\]
and the predicted velocity at impact is $v_{\mathrm{hit}} = v_b + [0, 0, -g\,\tau_{\mathrm{hit}}]^\top$. The desired paddle normal is computed from $v_{\mathrm{hit}}$ with a tilt gain ($0.22$) that angles the paddle against the ball's horizontal velocity component (up to $18^\circ$ maximum tilt) to maintain a centered bounce.

\textbf{Workspace and timing constraints.}
The predicted intercept must lie within the reachable workspace: $x \in [0.40, 1.00]$ m, $y \in [-0.55, 0.55]$ m, $z \in [0.22, 0.90]$ m. The prehit waypoint is placed $60$ mm before the contact point along the paddle normal, and the follow-through waypoint is $40$ mm past the contact point, ensuring a smooth swing trajectory through the contact point.

As a result, residual SAC learns \emph{how to improve} an already competent policy rather than learning ball bouncing from scratch.

\subsection{Inverse Kinematics Solver}
The IK stage within PLAN uses Drake's optimization-based \texttt{InverseKinematics} solver to convert Cartesian strike targets into feasible joint configurations for the 7-DOF KUKA iiwa arm. For each strike, the planner solves IK sequentially for three waypoints: prehit (approach pose), hit (contact pose), and follow-through (exit pose), seeding each solve from the previous solution.

The IK program enforces four constraint types:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Joint limits}: bounding-box constraints on all 7 iiwa joints, tightened to $\pm 1.40$ rad around the home configuration.
    \item \textbf{Seed window}: each joint is constrained within $\pm 0.65$ rad of the seed configuration, keeping solutions kinematically smooth between consecutive waypoints.
    \item \textbf{Position}: the paddle frame origin must be within $12$ mm of the target Cartesian point ($22$ mm in the relaxed fallback).
    \item \textbf{Orientation}: the paddle normal must be within $1^\circ$ of the desired strike normal ($2^\circ$ relaxed), computed from the incoming ball velocity direction with the tilt gain described above.
\end{enumerate}
A quadratic cost penalizes deviation from the seed configuration (weight 2.5 per iiwa joint), biasing solutions toward postures close to the current arm state. If the solver fails to find a feasible solution for any waypoint, the FSM transitions to RECOVER rather than executing an infeasible strike.

\subsection{Why Deterministic Evaluation Is Used}
Evaluation uses deterministic action selection (taking the mean of the policy's Gaussian output rather than sampling) to isolate policy quality from stochastic action sampling noise. This is particularly important when comparing checkpoints, because otherwise performance differences can be confounded by changing policy entropy realization.

However, deterministic evaluation also creates a limitation: under nominal fixed physics, some metrics become near-deterministic (e.g., near-zero standard deviation), reducing visibility into variability. We therefore include randomized evaluation and variance metrics to recover uncertainty information.

\subsection{MuJoCo Sim-to-Sim Transfer}

\section{Residual Scale Analysis}
\label{sec:residual_scale}

The residual scale parameter $\alpha$ controls how much of the learned SAC correction is injected on top of the FSM+IK baseline. At $\alpha = 0$, the system reduces to the pure FSM baseline with no RL contribution. As $\alpha$ increases, the RL agent exerts progressively larger joint-position corrections. This section analyzes how $\alpha$ affects performance in MuJoCo sim-to-sim transfer, revealing a narrow optimal range.

\subsection{Sweep Protocol}
We swept $\alpha$ over 15 values from $0.0$ to $0.014$ using the robust SAC policy in MuJoCo with 20 episodes per scale value (Table~\ref{tab:residual_sweep}). The sweep was conducted under nominal MuJoCo physics to isolate the effect of residual injection magnitude from physics perturbation.

\subsection{Key Findings}
\textbf{Optimal scale is narrow.} Peak performance occurs at $\alpha = 0.01$, achieving 30.4 mean hits---a 37.6\% improvement over the pure FSM baseline ($\alpha = 0$, 22.1 hits). Performance degrades sharply outside the $[0.007, 0.010]$ band (Figure~\ref{fig:residual_sweep}).

\textbf{Variance reduction.} Increasing $\alpha$ within the optimal range also reduces hit-count variability: standard deviation drops from 15.3 (at $\alpha = 0$) to 10.6 (at $\alpha = 0.01$), suggesting the residual correction stabilizes contact dynamics.

\textbf{Transfer gap explains the small optimal scale.}
The SAC policy was trained in Drake with $\alpha = 0.5$, but the optimal deployment scale in MuJoCo is $\alpha = 0.01$---a 50$\times$ reduction. This extreme attenuation reflects the sim-to-sim mismatch: corrections learned under Drake's contact model must be heavily dampened to avoid destabilizing the system under MuJoCo's different physics. At large $\alpha$ values, the RL corrections---well-calibrated for Drake---become counterproductive in MuJoCo, actively degrading baseline FSM performance.

\textbf{Practical implication.} When deploying a residual RL policy in a new simulator or on real hardware, the residual scale should be treated as a tunable transfer parameter rather than fixed at its training value. A simple grid search over $\alpha$ (as done here) provides an effective calibration mechanism.

\section{Failure Analysis and Lessons}
The original goal was a single policy that is best in absolute performance and robustness. Under the present budget, this did not happen. Two failure modes emerged:

\textbf{Failure mode A: Nominal over-specialization.}
Nominal SAC learns high-return trajectories but degrades under perturbation ($-32.0\%$ at 1M). This is consistent with specialization to fixed contact dynamics.

\textbf{Failure mode B: Robust under-optimization.}
Robust SAC improves relative robustness but remains below nominal SAC in peak absolute hits at 1M.

\textbf{Interventions attempted.}
We increased the training budget to 1M and retained intermediate checkpoint evaluation; used wide randomization ranges over contact and inertial parameters; and compared both training types under both evaluation conditions.

\textbf{Interpretation.}
Domain-randomized training appears to allocate capacity toward invariance across physics regimes, which may delay convergence to highly specialized nominal strategies.

\section{Threats to Validity}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Single-seed training}: uncertainty across random initializations is not fully characterized.
    \item \textbf{Deterministic nominal evaluation}: near-zero nominal std is expected but limits uncertainty reporting.
    \item \textbf{Simulation-only conclusions}: sim-to-real transfer is not yet validated on hardware.
    \item \textbf{Metric coupling}: hit counting and horizon settings influence rankings.
    \item \textbf{Residual scale sensitivity}: the optimal deployment scale ($\alpha = 0.01$ in MuJoCo vs $\alpha = 0.5$ in training) highlights that transfer requires careful recalibration.
\end{itemize}

\section{Model-Family Case Analysis}
\subsection{Nominal SAC Family}
The nominal SAC checkpoints illustrate a specialization trajectory. At 500k and 750k, the policy has moderate hit throughput and strong degradation under perturbation. At 1M, the policy reaches a qualitatively different regime with very high hit counts in both nominal and randomized tests, but with substantial variance under randomized physics ($\sigma = 106$).

This pattern suggests the learned strategy eventually discovers a high-frequency control mode that strongly exploits nominal simulator structure. The same strategy can still perform well on average under perturbation, but instability increases, consistent with brittle contact sensitivity.

\subsection{Robust SAC Family}
The robust SAC family behaves differently. Early checkpoints are conservative and low in absolute hits, then later checkpoints improve relative perturbation behavior (positive $\Delta$). This can be interpreted as the policy gradually internalizing dynamics-invariant corrective behavior.

Although robust 1M underperforms nominal 1M on absolute hit count, it is less volatile under randomized physics ($\sigma = 30.9$ vs $\sigma = 106$). For applications prioritizing reliability under model mismatch, this may be preferable despite lower peak returns.

\subsection{Baseline vs Learned Controllers}
The FSM+IK baseline remains a useful anchor because it is:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
    \item interpretable (state-machine phases and IK objectives are inspectable),
    \item stable in nominal deployment,
    \item reasonably robust under moderate perturbation ($-8.6\%$ degradation, Table~\ref{tab:main_results}).
\end{itemize}
Residual learning should therefore be judged not only by raw score uplift, but by whether it preserves or improves these baseline reliability properties.

\subsection{Practical Selection Guidance}
Given current evidence, a deployment-oriented selection rule could be:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
    \item choose \textbf{Nominal 1M} when maximizing expected throughput in environments close to nominal simulator dynamics;
    \item choose \textbf{Robust 1M} when dynamics uncertainty is large and performance consistency is prioritized;
    \item retain \textbf{FSM+IK} as a fallback safety baseline.
\end{itemize}
This policy-selection framing is often more actionable than a single ``best model'' declaration.

\section{Conclusion}
This project demonstrates a rigorous hybrid-control study: residual SAC significantly improves baseline performance in Drake, with nominal SAC achieving $3\times$ the FSM baseline hit count at 1M training steps.

\textbf{Domain randomization helps.} The robust SAC policy, trained with per-episode physics randomization, achieves positive perturbation effects ($+48.5\%$ at 1M), meaning it actually \emph{benefits} from randomized test conditions rather than degrading. This is in line with the theoretical expectation that domain-randomized training produces policies whose corrections generalize across the physics distribution rather than overfitting to a single simulator configuration.

\textbf{Consistency advantage.} Beyond mean performance, domain randomization dramatically reduces variance: robust SAC 1M exhibits $\sigma = 30.9$ under randomized physics compared to $\sigma = 106$ for nominal SAC 1M---nearly $3.5\times$ lower variance. For deployment scenarios where unpredictable performance swings are unacceptable, this consistency is arguably more important than peak hit count.

\textbf{Residual scale as a transfer knob.} The residual scale analysis (Section~\ref{sec:residual_scale}) reveals that the degree of RL injection must be carefully calibrated when transferring between simulators. In MuJoCo, the optimal scale ($\alpha = 0.01$) is $50\times$ smaller than the training value ($\alpha = 0.5$), and within this narrow optimal range, the RL residual improves FSM baseline performance by 37.6\%. This finding underscores that residual RL policies should not be deployed at their training-time injection magnitude in new environments; instead, the residual scale should be treated as a tunable transfer parameter.

The main scientific outcome is a data-backed trade-off between nominal specialization and perturbation robustness, supported by quantitative evidence from two simulators (Drake and MuJoCo), a residual scale analysis, and a comprehensive evaluation protocol.

\subsection{Proof of Residual Boundedness (Proposition 1)}
\begin{proposition}[Residual boundedness]
\label{prop:boundedness}
If $q_t^{\mathrm{base}}\in\mathcal{Q}$, then $q_t^{\mathrm{cmd}}\in\mathcal{Q}$ for all $t$ and for any policy output $a_t$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $q_t^{\mathrm{base}} \in \mathcal{Q}$ be the baseline joint command produced by the FSM+IK controller, where $\mathcal{Q} = \{q \in \mathbb{R}^7 : q_i^{\min} \le q_i \le q_i^{\max},\; i=1,\ldots,7\}$.

The residual action $a_t \in \mathbb{R}^7$ is first clipped: $\bar{a}_t = \mathrm{clip}(a_t, -1, 1)$, so $\bar{a}_t^{(i)} \in [-1, 1]$ for each joint $i$.

The unclamped command is:
\[
\tilde{q}_t = q_t^{\mathrm{base}} + \alpha \cdot \delta \cdot \bar{a}_t,
\]
where $\alpha \in [0,1]$ and $\delta = 0.15$ rad. Each component satisfies:
\[
|\tilde{q}_t^{(i)} - q_t^{\mathrm{base},(i)}| = \alpha \cdot \delta \cdot |\bar{a}_t^{(i)}| \le \alpha \cdot \delta \le 0.15 \;\text{rad}.
\]
Thus, the residual perturbation is bounded per joint.

The final command is computed by projection:
\[
q_t^{\mathrm{cmd}} = \Pi_{\mathcal{Q}}(\tilde{q}_t),
\]
where $\Pi_{\mathcal{Q}}$ clamps each joint independently:
\[
q_t^{\mathrm{cmd},(i)} = \max\!\bigl(q_i^{\min},\;\min(q_i^{\max},\;\tilde{q}_t^{(i)})\bigr).
\]
By construction, $q_t^{\mathrm{cmd},(i)} \in [q_i^{\min}, q_i^{\max}]$ for all $i$. Therefore $q_t^{\mathrm{cmd}} \in \mathcal{Q}$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Expected-Risk Interpretation (Proposition~\ref{prop:expected_risk})}

\begin{proof}
Let $\xi_1, \xi_2, \ldots, \xi_K$ be $K$ i.i.d.\ samples from $\mathcal{P}_\Xi$, where each $\xi_k$ specifies the physics parameters for training episode $k$.

The expected return of policy $\pi$ under the physics distribution is:
\[
J_{\mathrm{robust}}(\pi) = \mathbb{E}_{\xi \sim \mathcal{P}_\Xi}\!\left[\mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim P_\xi, \pi}[R(\tau)]\right].
\]

During domain-randomized training, at each episode $k$ the environment samples $\xi_k \sim \mathcal{P}_\Xi$ and runs the policy to collect trajectory $\tau_k$ under transition kernel $P_{\xi_k}$. The empirical return is $R(\tau_k)$.

The Monte Carlo estimate of $J_{\mathrm{robust}}(\pi)$ is:
\[
\hat{J}_K(\pi) = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K} R(\tau_k).
\]

Since each $\xi_k$ is i.i.d.\ from $\mathcal{P}_\Xi$ and each $\tau_k$ is sampled from $P_{\xi_k}$ under $\pi$:
\[
\mathbb{E}[\hat{J}_K(\pi)] = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K} \mathbb{E}_{\xi_k \sim \mathcal{P}_\Xi}\!\left[\mathbb{E}_{\tau_k \sim P_{\xi_k},\pi}[R(\tau_k)]\right] = J_{\mathrm{robust}}(\pi).
\]

Therefore, $\hat{J}_K(\pi)$ is an unbiased estimator of $J_{\mathrm{robust}}(\pi)$. By the law of large numbers, $\hat{J}_K(\pi) \xrightarrow{K \to \infty} J_{\mathrm{robust}}(\pi)$ almost surely.

In practice, the policy $\pi_\theta$ is parameterized by neural network weights $\theta$ and optimized via SAC gradient updates using individual episode returns. Function-approximation error (from the critic and actor networks) and finite training episodes introduce bias relative to the true optimal $\pi^*_{\mathrm{robust}}$, but the training objective itself targets $J_{\mathrm{robust}}$.
\end{proof}
